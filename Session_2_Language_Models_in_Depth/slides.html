The Hook

“How does your phone know what word you want to type next?”
“How does Google Translate understand ‘bank’ in ‘I went fishing at the bank’ vs ‘I deposited money at the bank’?”

The core challenge: Computers see numbers, not meaning. A computer has no innate understanding that “dog” is closer to “cat” than to “quantum physics.”

We need to convert words into numbers that capture meaning — and that is exactly what embeddings do.

The Problem with One-Hot Encoding

Traditional approach: Represent each word as a vector with one “1” and the rest “0”.

"cat" = [1, 0, 0, 0, 0, ...] "dog" = [0, 1, 0, 0, 0, ...] "quantum"= [0, 0, 1, 0, 0, ...]
The fatal flaw: Every vector is equally different from every other. “cat” and “dog” are as different as “cat” and “quantum.”

We lose all semantic relationships. The geometry of the vector space tells us nothing about meaning. One-hot vectors are orthogonal — the dot product between any two distinct words is always zero.

The Big Idea: Distributed Representations

“A word is known by the company it keeps” — J.R. Firth (1957)

Instead of one “1” and many “0”s, we use a dense vector of small numbers.

"king" = [0.8, 0.3, -0.1, 0.5, 0.2, -0.3, ...] "queen" = [0.7, 0.4, -0.2, 0.6, 0.1, -0.2, ...]
  • Each dimension captures a latent feature
  • Words with similar meanings have similar vectors
  • Meaning is distributed across all dimensions

Like a map where similar concepts are nearby cities. “king” is near “queen,” “Paris” is near “France,” “malaria” is near “fever.”

This is called the embedding space or semantic space.

Word2Vec

Introduced by Mikolov et al. at Google (2013). A breakthrough method for learning high-quality word embeddings from large text corpora.

CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)

Predict the target word from its surrounding context words.

Context: "The ___ sat on the mat" Target: "cat"

Skip-gram

Predict the surrounding context words from the target word.

Target: "cat" Context: ["The", "sat", "on", "the", "mat"]

Training on billions of words produces high-quality embeddings that capture rich semantic relationships.

The Famous Analogy

king − man + woman = queen

Vector direction “man” → “woman” represents gender
“king” → “queen” follows the same direction

⇒ Paris − France + Italy = Rome

This proves embeddings capture relational meaning. The vector arithmetic works because the geometry of the embedding space aligns with semantic dimensions.

Limitations of Static Embeddings

The “bank” problem: Same vector for all meanings.
“river bank” and “savings bank” get the SAME embedding.
  • No context awareness — word meaning is frozen
  • Can't handle polysemy — words with multiple meanings collapse into one vector
  • Out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words are impossible to represent
  • Trained once and frozen — can't adapt to new domains or specialized vocabularies

Transition to Contextual Embeddings

What if the embedding could change based on surrounding words?

“I went to the bank to deposit money”
→ bank = financial institution
“I sat on the river bank to fish”
→ bank = riverside

This is the key insight behind BERT, GPT, and Gemma. Context matters — and embeddings should reflect that.

BERT & The Transformer Revolution

BERT: Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (Devlin et al., 2018)

  • Key innovation: Each word gets a different embedding based on its context
  • Uses the Transformer architecture (attention mechanism)
  • “Bank” in sentence A has a different embedding than “bank” in sentence B
Masked Language Modeling:
“I went to the [MASK] to deposit money” → Predict “bank”

How Contextual Embeddings Work

Input: "The bank approved my loan" Tokenizer ↓ [CLS] The bank approved my loan [SEP] Transformer Layers ↓ Context-dependent vectors "bank" here = financial (not riverside)
Input: "The river bank was muddy" Tokenizer ↓ [CLS] The river bank was muddy [SEP] Transformer Layers ↓ Context-dependent vectors "bank" here = riverside (not financial)

The same word “bank” produces different vectors depending on the surrounding context.

Gemma — Google's Latest

Gemma by Google DeepMind (2024) — built on the same architecture as Gemini.

  • Open-source — available in 2B and 7B parameter sizes
  • Uses contextual embeddings with subword tokenization (256K vocabulary)
  • Designed for accessibility and research
“The doctor treated malaria patients”
→ embeddings capture medical context
“The farmer treated crops with pesticide”
→ embeddings capture agricultural context

Real-World Example: Legal Translation in Yorùbá

Challenge: Translating legal documents from English to Yorùbá with accurate semantic preservation.

“The court hereby sentences the defendant to five years imprisonment”
  • Static embedding: Each word has one meaning — “court” could mean basketball court
  • Contextual embedding: “court” = legal context (not basketball)
  • Yorùbá legal terms need culturally appropriate embeddings
  • “court” → “kootu” (legal tribunal)

This is only possible with contextual embeddings that disambiguate meaning based on surrounding words.

Climate Application: Flood Early Warning

“Heavy rainfall expected in Lagos lagoon area”

Embedding captures relationships:

“rainfall” → “flood” → “warning” → “evacuation” “Lagos” → “lagoon” → “coastal” → “vulnerable”
  • System can find historical patterns: similar weather embeddings → past flood events
  • Enables proactive disaster response and early warning systems
  • Connects “heavy rainfall” to “flood risk” without explicit rules
